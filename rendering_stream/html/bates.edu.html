Bates College

  1. Historic Quad Welcome

    Welcome to Bates College, a national liberal arts college located in Lewiston, Maine's second-largest city. Bates was founded in 1855 with uncommon ideals: to be open to everyone, regardless of economic status, ethnicity, race, or gender. Today, we are a small and proud academic community that strives to honor our founding ideals as an intellectually curious, forward-looking, and inclusive college.

    We are currently on the Historic Quad, the jewel of the Bates campus. The Quad is filled with towering oak and maple trees — perfect for setting up a slackline or hammock — and is crisscrossed with paths where students meet and greet one another.

    Before we get started, let me explain the many ways you can explore our beautiful space.

    You can click on the forward facing arrow to start walking around.
    You can jump to the next stop by clicking on the "Next" button.
    Now, if you already know where you want to go, you can select any location by using the list OR by clicking directly on the map.
    Lastly, remember that you can explore any particular location in more detail by clicking the supplemental icons.

  2. Hathorn Hall

    *Hathorn Hall is our first and oldest campus building. In addition to a variety of popular classroom spaces, Hathorn is where professors who teach courses in English, mathematics, and neuroscience have their offices. Listed on the National Register of Historic Places, Hathorn was designed by *Gridley Bryant, an influential New England architect. Throughout the day, the Hathorn bell tolls to signal the start and end of classes. It also tolls to celebrate Commencement in the spring and the beginning of the academic year each fall. Over Hathorn's front steps is a quieter reminder of the time of day: a historic clock given to Bates by the Class of 1931.

  3. Gomes Chapel

    A beautiful house of prayer and meditation open to all, *Gomes Chapel offers solitude, serenity, and community. It's a place to lift your voice in song, a place for quiet meditation, and a place for us to have important public discussions, including our amazing Martin Luther King Jr. Day celebration each January. Inside, banners of the world's major faith traditions offer visual reminders of Bates' inclusive founding and mission, as well as the vibrancy of the college's Multifaith Chaplaincy. The chapel is named for the late *Rev. Peter J. Gomes, a Bates graduate, preacher, and Harvard theologian celebrated for advancing LGBTQ rights from the pulpit and as a best-selling author.

  4. 280 College Street

    Welcome to *280 College Street. This residence offers a variety of study and hangout spaces, including *Frank's Lounge, a large, quiet common room with a fireplace. Around 150 students, from first-years up to seniors, make this residence their Bates home, building friendships along the way. Two-eighty features single rooms and doubles, and each hallway flows toward a shared lounge, where big windows let in lots of natural light year-round. The second floor is reserved for first-year students, who live together in what we call a First Year Center. Each center has a Junior Advisor, an upper-class student who is your mentor, expert, and guide on what to expect at Bates.

  5. Libbey Forum

    When Libbey Forum opened a century ago, it was a place where students gathered to discuss books, hold public debates, and sing together, beginning our great a cappella tradition. Today, students are still the focus at Libbey, which is the home of the college's registrar and our generous program of financial aid. Bates meets 100 percent of your financial need through a combination of grant aid, loans, and student employment. At Bates, financial aid is also available for study abroad. Bates works closely with families to ensure that you graduate with the lowest possible student loan debt — a dollar total that's less than half the national average.

  6. Schaeffer Theatre

    Several venues, including Schaeffer Theatre, support Bates students wishing to explore theater and dance. Our arts culture is egalitarian, welcoming students who seek great depth by majoring in these academic fields as well as students simply wishing to further develop their interests and abilities. Schaeffer is 300-seat proscenium-style house, while *Gannett Theater is a smaller, more flexible venue, and the experimental *Black Box Theater is home to many student-directed plays. Next door is *Pettigrew Hall, home of the Brooks Quimby Debate Society, one of the world's premiere debating clubs. Also here is the state-of-the-art Digital Media Studios, where an expert staff is always available for friendly, one-on-one support.

  7. Dana Chemistry

    This is *Dana Chemistry Hall. Here or in another academic space on campus, a field of study will capture your imagination and interest and become your academic major. It's where you'll spend time studying in lounges and labs with your fellow majors. It's where your senior thesis comes alive, where you turn data and facts and disconnected ideas into something of your own: new knowledge that you share with the world. And, thanks to our strong student-faculty ratio — 10 students for each professor — you will have close contact with your professors along the journey, including opportunities to collaborate on world-class research that's published in academic journals.

  8. Ladd Library

    *Ladd Library is the academic heart of Bates; it's located in the heart of campus, too, a short walk from most residence halls and classrooms. Like any library, Ladd has a dizzying collection of printed and electronic resources. But what really makes the library tick are great people, and Bates' librarians and staff are eager to help maximize your valuable study time. An innovative shared catalog with *Bowdoin and *Colby colleges lets you virtually browse the stacks at all three colleges and have the book you need within hours. The library is also home of the inclusive and welcoming Academic Resource Commons, offering friendly, student-to-student tutoring and academic support services.

  9. Roger Williams

    In a sense, the world is inside *Roger Williams Hall. Roger Williams is where you can study any number of languages and cultures, including French, German, Japanese, Mandarin, Russian, and Spanish. The building also houses the Center for Global Education. The center provides information, support, and assistance to Bates' international students, who come from more than 60 countries worldwide, and also to Bates students planning to study abroad on one of 200 approved programs around the world. These off-campus opportunities include our own programs, such as Short Term courses each spring and semester-long programs in the fall, such as this year's course in Chile studying science and culture.

  10. Pettengill Hall

    *Pettengill Hall is the home of the social sciences, like history, economics, anthropology, and psychology. When you walk into Pettengill, you know where you're heading because there's a compass in the lobby floor! From there, walk a few steps north and you enter the expansive Perry Atrium, where a three-story window wall offers a commanding view of Lake Andrews. Elegantly appointed with *Thomas Moser furniture, the atrium is a favorite spot for quiet study alone or with friends; it also has an academic wild side: Each spring, Pettengill hosts the Mount David Summit, a great tradition where hundreds of students present academic research to faculty, staff, family, and friends.

  11. Lake Andrews

    As you walk around *Lake Andrews or enjoy a quiet moment of reflection at the *Keigwin Amphitheater, don't forget to look up: You might see a circling osprey trying to spy a meal in the water below. Lake Andrews teems with birds, fish, turtles and a variety of trees, grasses, and flowering plants along the shore. The wildlife doesn't disappear in winter. Come January, Lake Andrews hosts a great student tradition, the Puddle Jump. After a hole's cut in the ice, friends in wild costumes head onto the ice for a quick dip together, then hustle back to shore where a bonfire gives everyone a big warm up.

  12. Olin Arts Center

    Overlooking Lake Andrews, the *Olin Arts Center is where you'll find the Department of Art and Visual Culture, the Department of Music, and the *Bates College Museum of Art. From the privacy of music practice rooms and art studios to the expansive museum galleries and the acoustic excellence of the 300-seat concert hall, Olin Arts Center drives and supports creative expression at Bates. Museum exhibitions are inclusive, diverse, and thought-provoking, such as a recent show of contemporary art by artists from *Saudi Arabia. Each spring, the museum hosts the popular Senior Thesis Exhibition, featuring works by senior art majors and attended by professors, staff members, friends, and family.

  13. Ben Mays/The Village

    *The Residential Village typifies the wide variety of Bates housing options, from cozy *Victorian homes on tree-lined *Frye Street to modern *Kalperis and *Chu halls, with lots of common spaces for hanging out with friends. The Village comprises three residences with suites for four to six students, where you and your roommates each have a bedroom around a shared living room. The Village also houses the *Benjamin Mays Center, a popular venue for talks and concerts, including the intimate Village Club Series. The center honors *Benjamin Mays, a 1920 graduate known as the "Schoolmaster of the Civil Rights Movement" for his mentoring of Martin Luther King Jr.

  14. Garcelon Field

    Welcome to *Garcelon Field. With student residences on two sides and Commons on a third creating a stadium feel, Garcelon is a central, student-friendly athletic space that keeps Bobcats on the move. One of America's oldest college fields, Garcelon was built in 1899 when Bates students helped to clear a pasture with axes and shovels. In recent years, Bates donors funded a stunning renovation, adding *FieldTurf and state-of-the-art grandstands, press box, and lighting. While Garcelon is best-known as the home of Bobcat football and lacrosse, it's a place for every kind of sport or activity, day and night and nearly all year long, including club, intramural, and pickup games.

  15. Commons

    Students love that Bates has a single dining hall, known as Commons, and an unlimited meal plan. In Commons, everyone comes together for great food and fun mealtime conversation and friendship. Instead of an outside food service, Bates buys its own food — 30 percent locally — which is prepared by Bates chefs in an open-kitchen setting. This gives students a chance to talk with the folks who prepare their meals. Focused on sustainability, Bates diverts 82 percent of its food waste by composting, recycling, and sending scraps to local farmers, efforts that earned Bates a rare gold rating from the Association for the Advancement of Sustainability in Higher Education.

  16. Chase Hall

    *Chase Hall looks the part of a traditional New England college building, right to the top, where a weathervane carries the initials of Benjamin Bates, the college's founding benefactor. Inside, Chase is forward-looking, with programs that guide and support students. Here you'll find the Office of Intercultural Education, a community, social, and discussion space for students that underscores our commitment to inclusion and equity. Also here is Purposeful Work, an innovative program that's gaining national media attention for helping students explore career aspirations in ways that feel personally meaningful and lead to future success. Chase also houses the *Bobcat Den, the campus bistro open until 2 a.m. on weekends.

  17. Alumni Gym

    Many sports venues around the world strive to combine historic elegance with contemporary improvements. Alumni Gymnasium does it perfectly. Created in 1926, the gym underwent significant renovations in 2018, adding a new floor, stands, and other amenities. Alumni Gym is home to the men's and women's basketball teams and the women's volleyball team. With an intimate atmosphere, traditional brick walls, and bleachers just a few feet from the court, the gym is beloved by Bates athletes and feared by opponents. Located near the library as well as dining Commons, Alumni Gym connects to the Gray Athletic Building, a multipurpose space for sports, concerts, and community gatherings all year long.

  18. Kalperis/College Store

    Two new residences, *Kalperis Hall and *Chu Hall, are side-by-side on Campus Avenue, each offering innovative design solutions and spaces for every student personality and purpose. The buildings have intimate nooks, lounges with cool furniture, and large spaces like the Treehouse, a sprawling fourth-floor lounge in Kalperis Hall with sweeping views. As with all construction projects, the halls achieve LEED Silver equivalency for sustainability. Another innovation is the placement of two popular campus businesses, the *College Store and the package and print shop, on the first floor of Kalperis. Their presence has transformed this area into a busy campus hub where friends see each other and new friendships are made.

  19. Mount David

    *Mount David is a small hill next to campus that looms large in the imaginations and experiences of Bates students. Trails maintained by students lead to a rocky summit with great views. It's just 381 feet tall, yet the hilltop feels secluded — perfect to hang with friends, watch a lunar eclipse, or launch sky lanterns. One of our great traditions takes place on the night before Commencement, when graduating seniors climb Mount David to watch the morning sunrise together. For students who are beginning to explore the natural world, a trip up Mount David is a simple, small step that often leads to bigger outdoor adventures in Maine.

  20. Morse Mountain

    An hour's drive from campus finds you at the Bates-Morse Mountain Conservation Area, a Maine coastal treasure comprising 600 acres of woods, salt marshes, and one of the largest undeveloped barrier beaches on the East Coast. In the conservation area, Bates puts into practice ideals of environmental stewardship, education, and research. In our era of climate change, research by Bates professors and students has a global audience of scientists and policy experts. For example, geologist Beverly Johnson does climate-change research on how wetlands capture and store carbon dioxide. Adjacent to the conservation area is the Bates Coastal Center at Shortridge, providing overnight accommodations and research support for professors and students.

  21. Lindholm House

    This is *Lindholm House, home of the Bates Office of Admission, where our deans strive to help families have a positive experience as you explore Bates and, especially, to get to know you as an individual. As a school that attracts academic talent while challenging the dominance of standardized testing through compelling research, we are leader in the test-optional movement. As our tour concludes, I invite you to register for more information about Bates, schedule a campus visit, or apply. Or, you can look for the "Connect" link on our Admission website and reach out to your regional admission representative or a current student. We thank you for joining us!

  22. Harward Center

    As a national leader, the Harward Center for Community Partnerships helps students engage with the Lewiston-Auburn community through both academics and service. Each year, about half of all Bates students take a class that connects them with our local community, and many students undertake community-engaged research projects and senior theses. Last year, a professor and her students worked with seven different local organizations to address specific needs, from mapping public art in downtown Lewiston to working with Somali Bantu families who tend vegetable gardens for subsistence and for market. Through these partnerships, students develop the valuable intellectual, ethical, and personal skills needed for a fulfilling life as a global citizen.
