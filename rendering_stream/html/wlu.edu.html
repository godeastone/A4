Washington and Lee University

Washington and Lee University in Lexington, Virginia, is a nationally ranked, small, private, liberal arts university nestled in the mountains of western Virginia.  As one of the oldest schools in the nation, we believe in the education of the whole person, and value students who are intellectually curious, eager to engage with their communities, inclined to critical thinking and prepared to lead lives of consequence.

Spring Term

At W&L, the year is divided into two 12-week terms in the fall and winter followed by a four-week Spring Term.

These courses are designed to be transformative learning experiences with a goal for students to exclaim after taking a spring term course, "this course changed my life."

Spring Term courses are hands-on. Interdisciplinary. Intense. And anything but traditional.

Mock Convention

Since 1908, the students of Washington and Lee University have come together to create one of the most ambitious, non-partisan student political research projects in the country, the W&L Mock Convention.

Every four years, Mock Convention simulates the presidential nominating convention with the goal of predicting who the party out of power will nominate to run for President of the United States in the upcoming election.

Mock Convention encompasses 98% of the entire campus and includes faculty, staff and law students. Mock Con serves as the intersection of the W&L community, enabling students and alumni from all backgrounds and disciplines to unite while predicting the nominee.

The Outing Club

W&L was recently named one of the 10 best colleges for people who love the outdoors by Money magazine.

The Outing Club is the most popular club on campus. Students lead many activities, including backpacking, fly fishing, canoeing, kayaking, rafting, rock climbing, mountain biking, caving, skiing, and outdoor cooking. 

Incoming first-year students can choose to hike the nearby Appalachian Trail during a pre-orientation trip — for many, the introduction to four years of adventure.

The Honor System

Among the many features that distinguish W&L, the Honor System has the most consequential and enduring impact on the W&L community. 

The Honor System at W&L is entirely student-run. It is based on the fundamental principle that students will not lie, cheat, steal or otherwise violate the community’s trust. The result is an environment that is collaborative, not cutthroat, and where rational discourse is the norm rather than the exception.

This type of honor stays with you long after graduation, fostering a sense of personal integrity that will continue to guide your actions and distinguish you as a graduate of W&L.

Washington and Lee

  1. Historic Front Campus

    Welcome to Washington and Lee University. We begin our tour on the historic front campus. The centuries-old Colonnade is the original academic heart of our campus and was designated a National Historic Landmark in 1972. Newcomb, Payne, Washington, Chavis and Tucker halls house many of W&L's language and humanities departments. Our small classes are a hallmark of an outstanding liberal arts education. The classrooms along the Colonnade retain 19th-century charm, but are equipped to meet the demands of modern teaching and research. Opposite the Colonnade is Lee Chapel, completed in 1868, which serves as a central gathering place for significant events and notable speakers. Before we get started, let me explain the many ways you can explore our beautiful space.

    You can click on the forward-facing arrow to start walking around.
    You can jump to the next stop by clicking on the "Next" button.
    If you already know where you want to go, you can select any location by using the list OR by clicking directly on the map.
    Lastly, remember that you can explore any particular location in more detail by clicking the supplemental icons.

  2. Admissions Office

    From the patio outside the Ruscio Center for Global Learning, you can see the Gilliam Admissions House, the Reeves Center, the Watson Pavilion and the Science Center. The Reeves Center includes exhibit space for ceramics, porcelain and paintings from W&L's extensive art collection. The Watson Pavilion is home to our Japanese Tearoom, one of just a few in the country to be located on a college campus. Both are distinctive, hands-on teaching spaces. The Tearoom serves as a classroom and center for cultural activities by the East Asian Languages and Literatures Department, and our collections of art and history are used in classes by professors across campus.

  3. Ruscio Center for Global Learning

    The Ruscio Center for Global Learning is home to many of our world language programs and the Office of International Education. The center's atrium is used for lectures, events and informal study sessions. Over 60 percent of our students travel abroad for coursework, research, or internships during their four years, many through one of our popular faculty-led Spring Term Abroad courses. These trips are possible thanks to our distinctive academic calendar, which includes two 12-week terms and a four-week Spring Term. We are committed to educating global citizens, so our generous financial aid packages will travel with you when you go abroad.

  4. Science Center

    The Science Addition provides classrooms, labs and offices for most of W&L's STEM programs. It also houses a dedicated science library and the IQ Center, with printing, visualization and data-capture equipment that is available to W&L students regardless of their major. Our faculty are renowned researchers, but they're your teachers first and foremost. They are also your collaborators, with 60 percent of STEM majors engaging in directed research with a professor. W&L is one of only a few top national liberal arts colleges to offer programs in engineering and neuroscience, and our acceptance rates to graduate programs in medical fields are nearly twice the national average.

  5. Stemmons Plaza

    Stemmons Plaza runs behind the Colonnade, through the academic center of campus. Stemmons bustles with activity between classes, and you're sure to see a familiar face among our 1800 undergraduate students. W&L is distinguished by two beloved, long-standing traditions: our Honor System and our Speaking Tradition. Our student-run Honor System creates a community of trust that provides students with unparalleled freedom and responsibility. Our Speaking Tradition is a custom of greeting friends, professors and visitors on or off-campus. Together, these traditions create an atmosphere of warmth and civility that reflect our commitment to an inclusive community that values diversity of thinking, backgrounds, and interests.

  6. Leyburn Library

    Open 24 hours a day when classes are in session, Leyburn Library is one of the busiest places on campus. On the lower levels, you'll find stacks of books and study carrels packed with students at all hours of the night. The library is also home to CARPE, our teaching and learning center. Need help with business or technical writing? Looking for math or science assistance, or digital media expertise? CARPE ensures you'll have every opportunity to expand your skillset as you embrace new subjects and more challenging material. Our Special Collections archive contains rare artifacts used to provide hands-on learning experiences across academic disciplines.

  7. Reid Hall

    Reid Hall houses our Journalism and Mass Communications Department. W&L is the only top national liberal arts institution with an accredited journalism program. We were also the first U.S. college to offer journalism studies. With our Honor System and commitment to personal integrity, W&L is a natural home for annual institutes in journalism and media ethics. Our students gain hands-on experience producing a weekly cable news program from the building's in-house television studio, and additional professional experience through internships at national news outlets and communications firms around the country. Students from any major can write for the student newspaper or one of our literary magazines, academic journals or reviews.

  8. Huntley Hall

    W&L is the only top-tier liberal arts college to offer an accredited undergraduate school of business. Classes in the Williams School of Commerce, Economics, and Politics are open to students from all majors, and students in business and accounting are encouraged to augment their studies with coursework in the liberal arts to broaden their perspectives. Many double-major or minor in STEM or humanities fields. W&L students also have outstanding opportunities to gain practical, hands-on experience. The Washington Term Program combines coursework and internships in Washington, D.C., while student organizations manage more than $10 million of the university's endowment, consult for local nonprofits, and support new business start-ups.

  9. John W. Elrod Commons

    The Elrod Commons is W&L's student center, featuring a movie theater, radio station, fireside lounge, the university bookstore and our main dining hall. All first-year students carry a full meal plan, while sophomores, juniors and seniors can choose from a variety of dining options. Located on the third floor, the Office of Career and Professional Development helps place over 95 percent of W&L graduates in jobs or graduate school within nine months of graduation. The Office of Inclusion and Engagement provides programs that explore and celebrate the diverse backgrounds of our students, faculty and staff, and fosters a culture in which all members of the community feel fully welcome on campus.

  10. Cannan Green

    Cannan Green lies between the Elrod Commons, Graham-Lees dormitory and the Richard L. Duchossois Athletic and Recreation Center, which includes the campus fitness center. This open space is literally at the intersection of where you'll eat, sleep, and play. For campus-wide events, like reunions or graduation, you'll picnic here beneath a tent. In quieter times, you can learn to cast a fly with the Outing Club or participate in our lively intramural programs, like spikeball, kickball or volleyball.

  11. Graham-Lees Residence Hall

    Graham-Lees is one of two first-year residence halls on campus. 80 percent of rooms are doubles; the remaining 20 percent are singles. There are single-gender and co-ed halls. Halls consist of about 20 students who share a common resident adviser, or RA, and a peer counselor. Bathrooms are communal and single-gender. RAs work with Residential Life staff to provide programming throughout the academic year, and are a great resource for first-year students. Residence life plays a significant role in the First-Year Experience, a program run by the Dean of First-Year students to ensure that the newest members of our community are supported academically and socially from day one on campus.

  12. Gaines Residence Hall

    Gaines Hall is the second of our two first-year residence halls. Separated by this courtyard from Graham-Lees Hall, all 460 of our first-year students will live in close proximity to one another and just a short walk to academic buildings, the performing arts center, the indoor athletics and fitness facility, and dining hall. Programs for the First-Year Experience begin before classes start, with our week-long pre-orientation trips in outdoor adventure, community service or history and culture. Throughout the year, events ranging from comedy shows, concerts and free movies to day-long getaways to nearby cities or natural attractions ensure there's always something to do.

  13. Lenfest Center for the Arts

    The Lenfest Center for the Arts is the creative center of campus. Lenfest Hall features a 400-seat traditional theater and a 170-seat black-box theater. Over 100 student-staged events and professional performances are presented throughout the year. Wilson Hall features classroom spaces and a 300-seat concert hall, as well as rehearsal rooms for W&L musicians and ensemble groups, and studio space for art majors. The Staniar and Kamen Galleries host art exhibits throughout the year, including a senior thesis show for graduating seniors. Music, theater and arts programs and courses are open to students in all majors, offering everyone an opportunity to get involved.

  14. Wilson Field Bridge

    The Wilson Field Bridge connects two main sections of campus and is a busy thoroughfare for students heading to class from the third-year housing village or athletic fields. It's a great spot to view W&L's six sorority houses and the Woods Creek Apartments, or to watch a tennis match on the courts below. In addition to fraternity and sorority housing, W&L offers a wide selection of themed housing for sophomores, juniors and seniors. Themes are based on student input, with offerings including global service, sustainability, culture and heritage, or hobbies and interests.

  15. Woods Creek Apartments

    Students are required to live on campus for their first three years. Woods Creek Apartments are a popular option for sophomores, offering newly renovated 3, 4, or 5-bedroom units with kitchens and balconies. Common areas outside each building provide space for students to gather and relax, and the scenic location offers easy access to walking and running trails along Woods Creek. The apartments are a short walk to W&L's academic buildings and athletic facilities. Other upper-division housing options include the housing village, themed residences and Greek houses.

  16. School of Law

    The School of Law is Washington and Lee's only graduate program. With an enrollment of approximately 360 students per year, it is one of the nation's smallest top-tier law schools. It is located in Sidney Lewis Hall, near Wilson Field and the Natatorium. Undergraduate students can take courses in the School of Law or minor in Law, Justice and Society, an interdisciplinary legal studies program taught by both undergraduate and law faculty. Be sure to visit the School of Law's tour to learn more about Sidney Lewis Hall and legal education at W&L.

  17. Wilson Field

    Wilson Field is the centerpiece of W&L's Duchossois Outdoor Athletics complex. W&L sponsors 24 Division Three NCAA sports, as well as club and intramural teams. About 25 percent of students play a varsity sport. W&L is a regular recipient of the Old Dominion Athletic Conference's award for overall athletic excellence. Our athletics program supports our students as scholar-athletes, encouraging them to excel academically and to participate fully in the life of the university. On the court or the field, W&L athletes learn skills like leadership, sportsmanship, integrity and responsibility. Off the field, they can be found on stage and in the lab, volunteering in town, or studying abroad.

  18. The Village

    Located above Wilson Field, the Village is a popular housing option for juniors. Many watch athletic contests and cheer on their fellow Generals from their porches and balconies. The complex includes four-bedroom apartments and six-bedroom townhouses. Within the Village is a restaurant, pub, fitness center, outdoor courtyard and fire pit. Residents enjoy the feeling of living in a small community with great views of the surrounding mountains and the campus. W&L students are required to live on campus for their first three years, but most seniors move off campus. The majority live in downtown apartments or country houses within a five-mile radius.

  19. Liberty Hall Ruins

    Washington and Lee was founded as Augusta Academy in 1749, making it the nation's ninth-oldest college. In 1776, the academy's trustees changed its name to Liberty Hall Academy. Four years later the school was moved from its original location to the vicinity of Lexington, where in 1782 it was chartered. A limestone building was erected for the academy in 1793. In 1796 the school received its first major endowment--$20,000 worth of James River Canal stock—from President George Washington. In gratitude, the trustees changed the name to Washington Academy. While the limestone building burned in 1803, its ruins are preserved today as a symbol of the institution's past.

  20. Outing Club Barn

    The Outing Club is one of the most popular student organizations on campus, offering classes and outdoor activities throughout the year. While the club has an office in Elrod Commons, its heart is in the Outing Club Barn, which features a student-built bouldering wall and equipment to check out for all kinds of adventures. W&L students lead most of the club's activities, and take full advantage of W&L's beautiful natural setting to go backpacking, fly fishing, canoeing, rafting, rock climbing, mountain biking, caving and skiing. Outings vary in length from day trips to ten-day excursions, including our pre-orientation Appalachian Adventure and a perennially popular kayaking trip to the Florida Everglades.

  21. Corner of Washington St and Lee Ave

    Located on the corner of Washington Street and Lee Avenue, Mattingly House is home to two of our signature interdisciplinary programs--the Shepherd Poverty Program and the Mudd Center for Ethics. Across the street is Hillel House, W&L's center for Jewish life, which hosts a weekly Shabbat Dinner that's open to all students and the local community. Just beyond Hillel House is the Red House, home to our Women, Gender and Sexuality Studies program and LGBTQ+ Resource center. The resource center provides a variety of programming and support for the LGBTQ+ community, including a peer counseling program. It also sponsors W&L's annual Equality Gala.

  22. Downtown Lexington

    Lexington is frequently listed among the mid-Atlantic's best small towns. It has plenty of amenities and entertainment options for everyone — local coffee shops, restaurants, hand crafted ice cream and gelato, art galleries, shops and a movie theater. And the best part is that it's within walking distance from any point on campus. Further afield, the natural beauty of the Shenandoah Valley and Blue Ridge Mountains provides the perfect backdrop for four-season outdoor adventure, and we have the nation's only community-owned drive-in movie theater. The cities of Roanoke and Charlottesville are each only an hour away, and Richmond is just a two-hour drive.
